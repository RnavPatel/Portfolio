Color-me-Out

How can doodles and drawings become a collaborative world-building practice for the future of storytelling.

Role: Creative Technologist, Designer

Duration: 3 weeks

Tools: Interaction Design, Arduino, Rhino, Digital Fabrication

Color-scapes.

One of the best qualities of sketching and doodling as a child is filling up the page, regardless of craft or finesse. I wanted to emphasize this joyful experience of “drawing for drawings sake” by creating these mysterious devices that feed off of color and display it as light. That way, the more you color, the more there is to be perceived by these “color-scape computers.”

However, each of these devices have their own personalities that affect how they perceive color. One is optimistic and always displays the color under it as bright, one is grumpy and inverses the color he sees, and the last is greedy and will get angry if there is no color under him (white/black). Through these devices, viewers can experience their drawings in new ways.

Developing the device.

When thinking about the device, I wanted to create a “black-box” to bring out curiosity from the viewers – primarily children. This led to more abstracted geometries of the cylinder as this almost ouija board device interaction of pushing these computers around a drawing. When thinking of scale, I made it two-handed so that people would be immersed with the devices.

Physically Prototyping.

For the physical computing, I used an Adafruit color sensor, Arduino Uno, and Adafruit Neopixel. The flow of information worked that color taken from the color sensor (as RGB wavelengths) would then be mapped via a gamma table to produce similar color lights in the neopixel strip. Based on this flow of information, I crafted the device to fit the computing parts and support sensor functions (e.g the color sensor is isolated and has a 5mm depth difference which allows it to accurately see the color under it).

Childproof Design.

Because my intended audience is for children, I worked to make the device durable and easily openable for debugging purposes. This involved working with tolerances to make the entire device friction fit, so that it could hold its form when in use and require no glue for any components. The interior structure was designed to hold the color sensor in place against rapid movement and keep the arduino board itself stable.

Playtesting.

After I got my first device, I wanted to playtest it to inform how I should present this experience. The overall consensus was that the joy from the project was largely dependent on the colors/materials available, and the size of the paper. Having people color the entire page themselves was too time-consuming and the device lost appeal after the first use. This needed to be collaborative and have colors ready to scan/interact with, as well as more devices that had different interactions.

Future Considerations.

I would like to start experimenting with interactions between the devices themselves, possibly if they get close to each other their lights can affect each other, or maybe mixing colors of light on another device. While multiple people can use these devices, it is still fairly individual and I would like to see the opportunities for co-creation.
